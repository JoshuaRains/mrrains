Introduction

In this study I will be analyzing the sound production of myself and native speakers of the Mexican variety of Spanish. I will record myself speaking both English and my non-native language of Spanish to determine how my sound production differs from the target speakers whose accent I wish to adopt into my own speaking. In my reflection I will compare my vowel production between English and Spanish and compare my Spanish vowel production to that of the native speaker. One speaker I have selected is a female twitch streamer from Guadalajara, Jalisco, Mexico who provides longform content which will give plenty of acoustic data to contribute to this study. The other speaker comes from a website that seeks to archive the different accents of the Spanish speaking world. This speaker is a male also from Mexico.

Observations on my Spanish Vowels


From the figure 2 we can observe a few things. My Spanish /u/ sound is very close to that of the female native speaker. This sound is probably one of the easier ones for me to replicate due to its proximity to the /ʊ/ from the phonetic inventory of my native language.

A similar phenomenon occurs with my Spanish /e/ sound which I produce very closely to the /ɪ/ sound from English. This places it near where I would predict that the male /e/ sound would appear, following the pattern of reducing overall vowel space used in the mouth in comparison with the female speaker. It does, however, appear more raised than expected in the male speaker.

The /i/ sound is also fairly close to those of the native speakers, appearing nicely in-between them. It also appears that when speaking Spanish I attempt to bring the /i/ further back than in English.

The most obvious difference between my vowels and those of the native speaker are /o/ and /a/. While my /o/ can be seen as remotely close to the target, being too fronted, but still being in-between the two native speakers, I was shocked to find that the /a/ is being produced among the back rounded vowels.

These observations present a few areas that I need to work on to sound more like a native speaker. I should use my English /ʌ/ phoneme as a model for producing the Spanish /o/ as it is almost identical to the native speaker’s /o/ sound. I should also start using my English /æ/ sound as a model for the /a/ sound, as this would put it exactly between the two native speakers.

Consonants

The most obvious places of comparison between the consonants that I produce and those of the native speakers are the endings of certain words. In word /kaliðað/ for example the native speaker leaves off the final consonant. This is something not present in my speech, where the /ð/ is clearly pronounced [kaliðað], [kaliðaː].
